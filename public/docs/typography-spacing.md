# Typography and Spacing

When placing two designs side by side—one that looks bad and one that looks good—most people can easily tell which one is better. However, understanding **why** one design is superior to another is a more challenging task. The ability to articulate why text should be handled in a certain way, or why space is utilized as it is, comes from hours of study and experience in design. But do all those hours of practice really need to be put in from scratch? Or can we learn from the brave designers who have already done the work? The answer is yes, we can. That’s why this guide will cover six practical tips to help you improve your layouts with text and spacing.

## The Importance of Typography

Typography isn’t just about the font you choose; it’s about how you use that font. To make this easier to grasp, each of the following tips will include a practical example, showing a correction to a layout. By the end, you can judge for yourself which version looks better.

## Tip 1: Line Height

**Line height** is the space between lines of text. It’s one of the quickest ways to improve a design from bad to good. If we look at websites from well-known companies, you'll notice that all of their headings fall within a similar range when it comes to line height. For example:

- **Google** generally uses a line height of 1.2x the text size.
- **Dropbox** also uses around 1.2x the text size.
- **Uber** falls somewhere between 1.2x and 1.5x the text size.

One key point to remember is that line height usually decreases as the heading size increases. When it comes to body text, these companies tend to use a line height of 1.4x to 1.5x the text size, with the same principle applying—larger text sizes require smaller line heights.

To improve your designs with line height:

- Use 1.1x to 1.3x the text size for your headings.
- Use 1.3x to 1.5x the text size for your body text.

## Tip 2: Letter Spacing

**Letter spacing** refers to the distance between characters in your text. While it may seem insignificant, it can have a significant impact on your design. Professional-looking headings often use negative letter spacing.

You might wonder why fonts don’t come with negative letter spacing by default if it looks so good. The reason is that negative letter spacing is typically only suitable for headings. Using it in body text can decrease readability.

Here’s a general rule of thumb for letter spacing:

- For headings, use negative letter spacing, typically between -1% and -2%.
- If it looks too tight, reduce the negative spacing slightly.

## Tip 3: Text Alignment

**Text alignment** can cause significant issues in your designs if not handled correctly. If you've ever read a long paragraph that is center-aligned, you might have noticed that it takes longer to read. This is because your eyes have to find the start of each new line, which slows down your reading speed.

However, center-aligned text isn't always bad. It works well for headings and shorter text chunks. But avoid mixing alignments—such as having a center-aligned heading with left-aligned body text.

A general rule to follow:

- For text spanning more than three lines, use left alignment.
- Do not mix alignments between headings and body text.

## Tip 4: Text Width

In a UX study by the **Baymard Institute**, it was revealed that long lines of text are often perceived as intimidating and overwhelming by users. As a result, users are more likely to avoid reading long text, which can lead to a lack of understanding of a product or service, ultimately reducing conversions.

So, what is an ideal text width?

- For body text, aim to stay within 50 to 75 characters per line.
- On desktop screens, a width of around 600 pixels is a good benchmark.

## Tip 5: Hierarchy in Text

A common mistake in design is overusing text sizes to indicate hierarchy. This can make your design look messy, random, and unprofessional. In design, less is often more.

A simple solution:

- Stick to just two different font sizes.
- Use font weight and subtle color changes to indicate hierarchy instead of multiple text sizes.

## Tip 6: Spacing Related to Text Elements

**White space** is one of the most neglected aspects of design by new designers. As John Doe (apologies for the name butchering!) once said, “White space is to be regarded as an active element, not a passive background.” White space should be as carefully considered as your images, buttons, or animations—if not more so.

A practical approach to white space:

- Consider the relationship between elements. Elements that are more closely related should be positioned closer together.
- For example, if the distance between a heading and its related body text is 16 pixels, the distance between the heading and the unrelated body text above it should be 32 pixels.

## Conclusion

The best part about these tips is that you can apply them to your designs today. By understanding and implementing these principles, you can avoid the pitfalls that many new designers face.
